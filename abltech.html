Pulmonary Vein Isolation

Complete pulmonary vein electrical isolation (PVI) on an atrial level is the best documented target for catheter ablation.

Gerstenfeld et al J Cardiovasc Electrophysiol 2002;13:971:979, Verma et al. N Engl J Med 2015;372:1812-22, and 2016 ESC guidelines for AF.

ADDITIONAL LINES

...such as roof line and mitral annulus line;

Use the same concept as Cox-Maze procedure.

Complex Fractioned Electrograms (CFAEs)

are EGMs with highly fractionaed potentials or with very short cycle length (<120ms).

Correlate with areas of slowed conduction and pivot points of reentrant wavelets.

Nademanee et al. J Am Coll Cardiol 2004;43:2044–53.

CRYOBALLOON

  • Subzero temperature creates ice crystals within the cell and disrupts cellular mechanisms.
  • Same efficacy as RF in paroxysmal AF.
  • Less esophageal injury.

Packer et al. STOP AF. J Am Coll Cardiol 2013;61:1713–23.

Venogram of RUPV before and during CRYOBALLOON application.

WHICH TECHNIQUES REIGN SUPREME?

Pulmonary vein isolation (PVI) is the most studied and recommended by all societies.

PVI is recommended as the main strategy for both paroxysmal and persistent AF.

Extensive ablation does not translate into a better outcome.

Additional lines and/or CFAEs ablation, however, may be considered in a re-do procedure.

Scherr et al. Circ Arrhythm Electrophysiol. 2015 Feb;8(1):18-24, Verma et al. N Engl J Med 2015;372:1812-22, 2012 HRS guidelines for AF Ablation, and 2016 ESC guidelines for AF.

Periprocedural Anticoagulation

2014 ACC guidelines & 2016 ESC guidelines for AF.

  • Periprocedural anticoagulation is indicated regardless of the patient’s thromboembolic risk.
  • Adequate anticoagulation at least 3 weeks before the procedure and continue at least 2 months after the procedure.
  • With adequate anticoagulation, TEE, to exclude LAA clot before the procedure, is not mandatory; but may consider.
  • Long term anticoagulation should follow general recommendations , regardless of the presumed rhythm outcome.

CONTINUATION of warfarin strategy was associated with less events (bleeding and Stroke) than BRIDGING strategy.
Periprocedural NOAC caused less bleeding than warfarin.

Di Biase et al. Circulation. 2014;129:2638-2644 and Calkin et al. N Engl J Med 2017;376:1627-36.

COMPLICATIONS

Life-threatening Complications

  • Death: <0.2%
  • Esophageal Injury: <0.5%
  • Stroke: <1%
  • Cardiac Tamponade: 1-2%

Severe Complications

  • Pulmonary Vein Stenosis: <1%
  • Persistent Phrenic Nerve Palsy: 1-2%
  • Vascular Complications: 2-4%

Unknown Significance

  • Asymptomatic Cerebral Embolism: 5-20%
  • Radiation Exposure

Gupta et al. Circ Arrhythm Electrophysiol. 2013;6:1082-1088, and 2016 ESC guidelines for AF.

Notable Complications

PV narrowing could occur in 5% after PVI but severe stenosis (>75%) is rare.

Symptoms: dyspnea, cough, hemoptysis, recurrent lung infection.

Symptoms are unlikely without severe stenoses; even asymptomatic with complete occlusion.

Von Bary et al. Circ Arrhythm Electrophysiol. 2011;4:630-636 and Prieto et al. Heart Rhythm. 2010 Sep;7(9):1233-9.

PHRENIC NERVE INJURY

More common with Cryoballoon than RF.

Mostly transient; long-lasting >12 months is rare.

Squara et al. Circ Arrhythm Electrophysiol. 2014;7:561-562.

GASTROPARESIS

from vagal nerve denervation from ablation.

Mostly asymptomatic and transient.

Knopp et al. Heart Rhythm 2014;11:574–578.

ATRIAL ARRHYTHMIAS

Atyical/left atrial flutter is not uncommon after extensive ablation.

BLANKING PERIOD:

AF could occur upto 1/3 of patients during the first 3 months after the ablation due to the stimulatory effect of RF itself.

Leong-Sit P et al. Circ Arrhythm Electrophysiol. 2011;4(1):11–14 and 2012 HRS guidelines for AF ablation.
